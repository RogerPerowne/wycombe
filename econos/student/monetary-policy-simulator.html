You are a Member of the
Monetary Policy Committee

Bank of England · Nine Members · Eight Meetings per Year

The Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) meets eight times each year at Threadneedle Street to set the Bank Rate — the most powerful tool in the UK economic armoury. Your primary mandate, set by Parliament, is to maintain CPI inflation at 2%. You must also support the government's objectives for growth and employment, without prejudicing the inflation target.

Interest rates work through the monetary policy transmission mechanism: changes ripple outward from the Bank Rate through commercial borrowing costs, consumer spending, investment, the exchange rate, and ultimately — after an 18–24 month lag — feed through into inflation, growth, jobs, and trade.

Each scenario places you in a real moment of UK economic history. You will read a confidential MPC briefing, study the latest economic data, and set the Bank Rate. Your decision will be scored and explained. At the end, you will receive a personalised performance report.

📊 What you will see

Real-time indicators for inflation, GDP growth, unemployment, the current account, consumption, investment, and more — mirroring what the MPC actually studies.

⚖️ What you must decide

Set the Bank Rate using the slider. You may raise, hold, or cut. Consider all indicators — not just inflation. Real trade-offs between objectives exist.

💡 Hints available

Each scenario has a guided hint if you are stuck. Warning: using a hint deducts 10 points from that scenario's score.

📚 Key Theory
Transmission Mechanism
Rate changes flow via: market interest rates → asset & house prices → exchange rate → credit availability → expectations → net trade → and finally, inflation. Full effect takes 18–24 months.
Cost-Push vs Demand-Pull
Rate rises work against demand-pull inflation (excess spending). They are far less effective — and potentially harmful — against cost-push inflation caused by oil prices, supply chains, or a weak pound.
Exchange Rate Channel
Higher rates → capital inflows → stronger £ → cheaper imports → lower imported inflation. But also: exports less competitive → worsens current account deficit.
Conflicts between Objectives
Tightening to fight inflation can slow growth, raise unemployment, and worsen investment. The MPC must balance all four macroeconomic objectives simultaneously.
