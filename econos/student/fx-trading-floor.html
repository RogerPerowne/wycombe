The 25-Mark Essay Planner

Everything you've just simulated maps onto a single classic Edexcel evaluation question. Click each step to reveal the planning framework that earns A* marks. Use this structure when you tackle exchange rate questions in the exam.

Edexcel Paper 2 — essay-style question 25 marks
"Evaluate the likely impact of a sustained depreciation of the pound sterling on the UK economy."
01
Define & set up (KU marks)
click to reveal ↓
Open with crisp, accurate definitions to bank knowledge marks early.
  • Depreciation — a market-driven fall in the value of a currency in a floating exchange rate system. Note: not devaluation
  • Floating exchange rate — value determined by supply and demand on the foreign exchange market
  • SPICED reversed — Weak Pound, Imports Dear, Exports Cheap
  • Use a brief currency demand/supply diagram showing the supply of £ shifting right, equilibrium price falling
02
Build the analysis chains (KAA marks)
click to reveal ↓
Three analytical chains, each linking depreciation to a macro objective via clear cause-and-effect logic.
  • Chain 1 — Current account & growth: Depreciation → exports cheaper, imports dearer → (X-M) component of AD rises → AD shifts right → real GDP growth ↑ and employment ↑. Reference SPICED and the (X-M) component of AD.
  • Chain 2 — FDI inflows: Cheaper £ → UK assets cheaper for foreign investors → FDI inflows rise → long-run productive capacity ↑ → LRAS shifts right.
  • Chain 3 — Cost-push inflation: Weaker £ → imported raw material costs rise → SRAS shifts left → price level rises, output falls (cost-push inflation). Reference imported inflation.
03
Evaluate (AO4 marks — where A* is earned)
click to reveal ↓
Top answers don't just list points — they specify conditions, time horizons and magnitudes. Use these evaluation hooks:
  • Marshall-Lerner condition — current account only improves if |PEDx| + |PEDm| ≥ 1. UK's elasticities are higher in the long run than the short run.
  • J-curve effect — short-run worsening of the trade balance before long-run improvement. Specify the time horizon explicitly.
  • Falling real wages — imported inflation erodes purchasing power even as GDP grows. Distributional concern.
  • Depends on the cause of depreciation — hot money outflow, trade deficit, or speculation each have different policy implications.
  • Magnitude — a 5% depreciation has very different effects from a 25% one.
  • Real-world example — drop in 1992 ERM exit, 2016 Brexit fall, or 2022 mini-budget crisis for evidence.
04
Conclude with judgement (AO4 marks)
click to reveal ↓
A strong conclusion makes a justified judgement, not just a summary. Use the "it depends on" framework:
  • It depends on the time horizon — short-run J-curve worsening, long-run improvement (subject to Marshall-Lerner).
  • It depends on the cause — depreciation from a competitiveness gain is positive; depreciation from a fiscal crisis (e.g. 2022) signals lost confidence.
  • It depends on whether ML holds — without it, depreciation does not improve the current account.
  • It depends on the policy response — if the central bank raises rates to defend the pound, the export benefit is unwound.
  • Net judgement — overall, a moderate depreciation is likely to support growth and the current account in the long run, provided inflation expectations remain anchored. A sharp, disorderly depreciation (as in 2022) is more likely to be net-negative.
Exchange Rate Glossary — 4.1.8
Depreciation
Market-driven fall in a floating exchange rate. Caused by changes in supply and demand for the currency.
Devaluation
Deliberate policy lowering of a fixed exchange rate by the government or central bank.
Appreciation
Market-driven rise in a floating exchange rate.
Revaluation
Deliberate policy raising of a fixed exchange rate.
Hot money
Short-term internationally mobile capital that flows toward higher-yielding currencies. Moves through the financial account.
Marshall-Lerner condition
A depreciation improves the current account only if |PEDexports| + |PEDimports| ≥ 1.
J-curve effect
Following depreciation, the current account worsens in the short run before improving in the long run, as trade volumes adjust slowly.
SPICED
Strong Pound, Imports Cheap, Exports Dear. Reverse for a weak pound.
Cost-push inflation
Inflation caused by rising input costs (e.g. imported raw materials after a depreciation), shifting SRAS left.
Demand-pull inflation
Inflation caused by rising AD (e.g. higher exports after depreciation pushing AD right).
Current account
Section of the balance of payments recording trade in goods, services, primary income (interest, profit, dividends) and secondary income (transfers).
Financial account
Section of the balance of payments recording flows of investment capital — FDI, portfolio investment, hot money, reserves.
Floating exchange rate
Exchange rate determined entirely by market forces of supply and demand. The UK has used this since 1992.
Managed float
Hybrid system: market-determined but the central bank intervenes occasionally (e.g. India, Japan).
Fixed exchange rate
Government/central bank pegs the currency, defending the rate via reserves and interest rates.
Competitive depreciation
Deliberate weakening of a currency to boost export competitiveness. Risks retaliation and currency wars.
Foreign exchange reserves
Stock of foreign currencies held by a central bank, used to intervene in forex markets and defend the exchange rate.
FDI
Foreign Direct Investment — long-term investment by a foreign firm to gain a controlling interest in a domestic enterprise.
