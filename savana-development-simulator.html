🌍 Country Profile

Savana is a landlocked nation of 18 million people in sub-Saharan Africa. It is heavily dependent on copper exports, whose price is set by global markets you cannot control. A 1990s IMF structural adjustment programme left deep debt and a gutted public sector.

⚙️ How It Works

Each year you set a tax rate (which determines your government budget) then allocate that budget across 8 fixed investment categories. You also face one special decision unique to that year. Random shocks will hit — you cannot predict them, only prepare.

🏆 How You Are Scored

Each year is scored on five outcomes — not inputs. HDI improvement (30%), GDP growth (20%), inequality reduction (20%), debt sustainability (15%), and political stability (15%). Time lags mean education investment pays off slowly; debt neglect compounds painfully.

You have won the election on a platform of change. Your people are poor. 67% lack reliable electricity. Secondary school completion is below 28%. Life expectancy is 58. The infant mortality rate is among the highest on the continent.

You inherit a government that collects only 16% of GDP in taxes — not enough to fund the transformation your country desperately needs. But raise taxes too high and you deter the private investment that creates jobs. This is the first of many trade-offs you will face.

The IMF is watching. The World Bank is watching. Your people are watching. You have ten years.

📚 Key Concepts
Harrod-Domar Model
Growth = savings rate ÷ capital-output ratio. Low income → low savings → low investment → poverty trap. FDI and aid plug the gap — but require absorptive capacity (skills, institutions).
Lewis Dual-Sector Model
Agricultural surplus labour shifts to industry, raising productivity. Requires infrastructure and investment in manufacturing capacity to absorb the labour productively.
Institutions (Acemoglu)
Inclusive institutions (property rights, rule of law, anti-corruption) attract investment and enable broad-based growth. Extractive institutions concentrate gains among elites.
Laffer Curve & Tax
There is an optimal tax rate beyond which revenue falls as activity is deterred. Low-income economies often under-tax — but very high rates deter FDI and private enterprise.
Debt Sustainability
Debt is manageable when growth (g) exceeds the interest rate (r). Above 80% debt/GDP, lenders demand risk premiums that crowd out development spending.
